# Unofficial MEXT Worldwide
## Welcome!

This website's main purpose is to bring together information about the several MEXT scholarships available in one convenient place. Though, it mainly mirrors the information channels from our [Discord server](https://discord.gg/kzND3sX3zq). If you want to dive deeper into the details, ask questions, and talk to other applicants, I highly suggest you join the server.

> General warnings: Pay close attention to the scholarship you want to pursue and the current political situation in Japan. Recently, several university-funded programs have started to reduce the number of students, reduce their stay, or shut down completely. For the time being, it's safe to assume that the Monbunkagakusho Funded Programs are not going to be affected.

# 1. What is MEXT?

MEXT stands for the "Ministry of Education, Culture, Sports, Science and Technology" of Japan. However, whenever you hear the acronym by itself, it more likely than not refers to the Japanese Government MEXT Scholarships.

# 2. How do I apply?

First, you need to search your local embassy's or consulate's website. [This website](https://www.mofa.go.jp/about/emb_cons/mofaserv.html) provides a list of embassy and consulate websites from all over the world, so I recommend starting there.

You should also take a look at the [Study in Japan website](https://www.studyinjapan.go.jp/en/planning/scholarships/mext-scholarships/). They are the main source of official information other than the embassy websites. You can also download the formulary and required documents in their website.

After that, you should decide on which scholarship you want to apply to. Depending on your consulate, a variety of scholarship types may be offered.

# 3. What Scholarships are Available?

After figuring out whether your country offers a scholarship, you should decide which scholarship type fits you the best. A summary of each of them is described as follows:

- **Undergraduate [UG]:** A scholarship offered to students pursuing a bachelor's degree.
- **Special Training College [STC]:** Equivalent to a vocational school.

- **College of Technology [CoT]:** Allows the student to enter a Higher Technical School's 3rd year after a preparatory year.

- **Research / Postgraduate [PG]:** A scholarship offered to students pursuing a master's or a doctoral degree.

- **Culture and Japanese Language [CJL]:** A scholarship offered to students to enhance their knowledge of Japanese language, literature, and culture.

- **School Professor Training [SPT]:** A scholarship offered to students to enhance their knowledge of Japanese language, literature, and culture.

As previously mentioned, different embassies and consulates offer different scholarship types, so check whether it offers the one you are interested in.

# 4. Am I eligible?

The [Study in Japan website](https://www.studyinjapan.go.jp/en/planning/scholarships/mext-scholarships/) provides a table with the requirements for each scholarship, but your consulate/embassy may have additional requirements.

Most scholarships may have different requirements, especially in regards to age and nationality.
However, most of them follow a certain pattern:

- Must have the same nationality as the country of the scholarship;
- Applications with dual japanese nationality are generally not allowed;
- Advanced knowledge of English or Japanese (if you get a zero in Japanese, you're immediately disqualified from any scholarhip);
- Availability to depart in the first week of April the year after the application;
- Physically and mentally fit to study at a Japanese institution;
- Generally, active duty military personnel is not allowed;
- People expecting to receive any other scholarship are not allowed;
- People already enrolled in a Japanese university under the "Student" visa are not allowed.
- People who have already received a MEXT scholarship in the past are not allowed.

# 5. Language Requirements

> TL;DR: For humanities-related fields: JLPT N2+; For natural sciences: N5 is not required, but advised.

> Note: once again, not all consulates or embassies work the same. If you have any questions, send them an email.

In general, it's expected that the applicant has a basic understanding of the Japanese language, that is, N5 level (or even being able to read kana) is a minimum.

Professor Training applicants are expected to be fluent in Japanese.
Culture and Japanese Language applicants, and any other scholarship that is related to humanities are also required to have a high level of understanding of the Japanese language (N2 level and above).

As for English, having a C1-C2 level is a must. Most consulates/embassies require an English Proficiency Test Certificate.

If you don't have a certificate, it doesn't hurt to try the [EF Set](https://www.efset.org), although some consulates may not accept it. The Duolingo English Test is also an affordable option if you're currently enrolled in an university that accepts it. You can also ask the administrative sector to get a license so you can take the exam for free.

# 6. Field of Study and Course Major
The [Study in Japan](https://www.studyinjapan.go.jp/en/) website provides a list of [Major Codes](https://www.studyinjapan.go.jp/en/_mt/2022/07/major_codes.xlsx) and a [table of graduate schools, universities, and junior colleges](https://www.studyinjapan.go.jp/en/_mt/2021/11/School_Search_20200512.xlsx) you can attend.

**Points to note:**
- **Undergraduate** students are only allowed to enroll in Japanese National Universities (国立大学). At the end of your preparatory year, you will have to write down a list of universities you want to attend (up to 23x). CJLC and MEXT will then try to get in contact with the university, and you may or may not be required to undergo additional documentation stages, interviews and/or examinations.
- **Special Training** students have an even more limited amount of schools they can attend to.
- **College of Technology** students also have their own enrollment requirements.

With that said, take those tables with a grain of salt.

# 7. Internal Procedures & Confidentiality
This scholarship has a bad reputation for being quite restrictive. If you are rejected, more likely than not, you will **not** receive an adequate feedback as to why that decision has been taken, especially if you are from a more competitive country.

Additionally, the consulates and embassies are not allowed to give you exam scores. The ones who do are not acting according to the regulations.

The Undergraduate scholarship in specific is known for being the most sought-after, and therefore the hardest to pass in most countries. 

That said, always have a backup plan. _This scholarship is basically a gacha._
